AutoPK: Automated Pharmacokinetic Parameter Extraction from Scientific Tables

Manuscript in Preparation

Problem Statement

Extracting structured pharmacokinetic parameters from scientific literature requires processing heterogeneous tables with inconsistent formatting, merged cells, and varied parameter naming conventions. Direct application of large language models produces hallucination rates of 60-95%, making automated extraction impractical for safety-critical applications like veterinary drug dosing.

Technical Contribution

Developed a two-stage ML framework that reduces hallucinations through structured decomposition. Stage 1: Hybrid similarity algorithm combining Bio+Clinical BERT embeddings (semantic), Levenshtein distance (lexical), and Sørensen-Dice coefficient (token overlap) to identify parameter variants across 338 half-life, 602 AUC, and 370 clearance naming variations. Stage 2: Few-shot prompting with table simplification to reconstruct standardized CSV outputs.

Key Innovation: Structured preprocessing enables smaller open-source models (Gemma 3-27B) to outperform larger commercial models (GPT-4o Mini) on domain-specific extraction tasks, demonstrating that architectural design can compensate for model size limitations.

Results & Impact

Performance: AutoPK with LLaMA 3.1-70B achieved F1-scores of 0.92 (half-life) and 0.91 (clearance), outperforming direct LLM baselines by 0.10-0.21 margins. Hallucination rates dropped from 60-95% to 8-14%. Gemma 3-27B with AutoPK showed 2-7 fold F1-score improvements over direct usage.

Real-World Impact: Supports the Food Animal Residue Avoidance Databank (FARAD) program serving thousands of veterinary stakeholders annually. Addresses a 40+ year manual curation backlog, enabling more timely food safety guidance.

Physics-Informed Neural Networks for Molecular Structure Retrieval from Coulomb Explosion

Manuscript in Preparation

Problem Statement

Retrieving molecular structure from Coulomb explosion imaging requires solving an inverse problem: mapping final ion momenta to initial atomic positions. This problem exhibits multi-modality (rotational/translational degeneracies) and benefits from incorporating physical constraints (energy conservation). The challenge is determining which neural network architectures effectively leverage domain knowledge versus purely data-driven approaches.

Technical Contribution

Systematic comparison of seven neural network architectures across two categories: (1) Generative models: VAE, Normalizing Flows (Real NVP), Neural Posterior Estimation, Mixture Density Networks, Diffusion Models; (2) Physics-informed models: PIRR (Physics-Integrated Residual Regression) and PINNs with energy conservation penalties.

Key Methodological Insight: Coordinate preprocessing to remove nuisance degrees of freedom (reducing dimensionality from 15 to 12) improved VAE performance by 40%, demonstrating that architectural design must account for physical symmetries. Generated datasets of 10K and 1M samples using RK45 ODE solvers to simulate forward Coulomb explosion dynamics.

Results & Applications

Performance: On 1M sample dataset, Normalizing Flows achieved R² = 0.870 (best generative model), while PINN reached R² = 0.829 with energy conservation constraints. Physics-informed models showed stronger performance on small datasets (10K samples), suggesting value of domain knowledge when data is limited.

Applications: Applied to polyhalomethane isomers (CHBrClF), achieved per-atom position errors below 0.3 atomic units, enabling automated structure discrimination for isomer identification. Enables automated molecular structure determination from experimental Coulomb explosion data for ultrafast photochemistry studies, photoisomerization dynamics, and non-adiabatic processes.

Diagnosing Machine Learning Failures in Multi-Environment Agricultural Prediction

Manuscript in Preparation

Problem Statement

Machine learning models for agricultural yield prediction often fail when deployed across different environments (locations, years, soil types). Existing approaches focus on algorithmic sophistication—more complex models, automated feature selection, deep learning—yet practical deployment remains unreliable. The fundamental question: What determines whether a model will generalize across environments?

Technical Contribution

Exhaustive Evaluation: Evaluated seven ML methodologies across 18,660 train-test configurations (9 environment-experiment combinations spanning 2 years, 3 locations, 180 total observations). Compared manual ensemble (Extra Trees, Random Forest, Ridge with domain-informed features), adaptive feature selection, dimensionality reduction (PCA, SVD), target transformation, and regularized regression.

Key Innovation: Developed correlation structure diagnostics that identify when models will fail before deployment by analyzing feature-target correlation sign consistency between training and test sets. This provides a practical deployment framework: use diagnostics to identify when new data collection is required rather than pursuing more complex models.

Key Findings & Impact

Performance Results: Parsimonious ensemble with two domain-informed features (Thermal, BNDVI) achieved mean R² = 0.016 across 18,660 configurations, outperforming all automated methods. Sequential integration showed non-monotonic returns: adding biochemical traits improved R² to 0.220 (13.75×), but pollen metrics degraded performance to R² = 0.184 (law of diminishing returns).

Diagnostic Validation: Correlation diagnostics achieved 100% accuracy in distinguishing high-performing (R² = 0.54) from failing configurations (R² = 0.79 with high RMSE = 2.79), revealing that correlation structure consistency is necessary for cross-environment generalization.

Broader Impact: Established that data quality (correlation structure consistency) supersedes algorithmic sophistication for multi-environment problems. Applies beyond agriculture to any heterogeneous multi-environment prediction domain (healthcare across hospitals, manufacturing across facilities, climate models across regions).

Research Philosophy

My research follows a consistent methodology: (1) identify where current ML approaches fail on real-world problems, (2) understand why they fail through systematic evaluation, (3) develop solutions that combine domain knowledge with algorithmic innovation, and (4) establish diagnostic frameworks to predict when methods will work. This approach prioritizes understanding over performance chasing, and practical deployment over benchmark optimization.

Open Science Commitment: I am preparing these projects for publication with plans to release code, data, and models publicly. I believe computational research should be reproducible and that open-source contributions accelerate scientific progress.
